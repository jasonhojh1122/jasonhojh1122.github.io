Competition Panel (Ghiberti)

Lorenzo Ghiberti Bronze relief 1401

Ghiberti's winning competition panel for the second set of bronze doors for the Florence Baptistery. Only the Isaac figure was cast separately, making the whole piece lighter but stronger.

Competition Panel (Brunelleschi)

Filippo Brunelleschi Bronze relief 1401

Brunelleschi's competition panel for the second set of bronze doors for the Florence Baptistery. The servant in the left's pose is derived from the Spinario, a popular Roman sculpture. Each figure was cast individually.

St. George and the Dragon

Donatello Marble relief 1416–1417

Predella relief originally below the St. George statue. Features a new approach that focuses on light and shadows, letting the figure blend into the background.

St. George

Donatello Marble 1416–1417

Bronze David

Donatello Bronze c. 1440s

First large-scale nude sculpture since antiquity. The first unsupported standing work of bronze cast during the Renaissance, and the first freestanding nude male sculpture made since antiquity. The pose is derived from contrapposto, with the left knee coming forward against the cloth. David is depicted with an enigmatic smile, posed with his foot on Goliath's severed head.

Atys-Amorino

Donatello Bronze c. 1440

Bronze sculpture combining attributes of Eros and Attis.

David

Andrea del Verrocchio Bronze c. 1473–1475

Bronze sculpture depicting David as a young man, with leather cloth and a smiling expression instead of the uncertain expression seen in earlier depictions.

Portrait of a Lady with Flowers

Andrea del Verrocchio Marble c. 1475–1480

First three-quarter-length sculpted portrait since antiquity. A landmark in Renaissance portraiture.

Hercules and Antaeus (Bronze)

Antonio del Pollaiuolo Bronze c. 1475

Bronze sculpture depicting Hercules wrestling with the giant Antaeus. Shows Pollaiuolo's mastery of depicting dynamic action in three dimensions.

Bacchus

Michelangelo Marble 1496-1497

A marble sculpture of the Roman god of wine, depicted nude with vine leaves and grapes, his body swaying as if affected by alcohol. Michelangelo was paid 160 florins for this work. The sculpture demonstrates his ability to capture both classical ideals and naturalistic observation.
