Assumption of Mary

Wikipedia

Summary

The belief that Mary, mother of Jesus, was assumed bodily into Heaven at the end of her earthly life. The subject of the Frari's dedication and Titian's famous altarpiece.

Biblical Reference

Book(s): Catholic doctrine (not explicitly in canonical scripture)

Vallombrosa Altarpiece Self-researched

Pietro Perugino Oil on panel 1500-1501

Also known as the Assumption of the Virgin, this glittering altarpiece features a three-layered composition with God the Father at the top surrounded by cherubs and angels, the Virgin Mary ascending in the middle, and saints below. One of Perugino's major works.

Assumption of the Virgin Self-researched

Titian Oil on panel 1515-1518

Titian's most famous painting, commissioned for the Basilica and remaining in its original position on the high altar. The largest altarpiece in Venice, with figures well over life-size. The work established Titian as the most prominent painter of 16th century Venice. Also known as the Assunta.

Panciatichi Assumption Self-researched

Andrea del Sarto Oil on panel c. 1522–1523

A majestic Assumption of the Virgin painted for the Panciatichi family, showing the Virgin Mary ascending to heaven surrounded by angels, with apostles gathered around the empty tomb below. The work demonstrates Andrea del Sarto's refined spatial composition and experimental use of color that influenced the next generation of Mannerist painters.

Sforza Chapel Self-researched

Michelangelo Architecture with travertine and plaster c. 1564–1573

Designed by Michelangelo near the end of his life and completed posthumously by Tiberio Calcagni and Giacomo Della Porta. The chapel features an innovative elliptical plan with a three-part vault and curving side apses, representing one of Michelangelo's most forward-looking architectural experiments.

Sistine Chapel Frescoes (Santa Maria Maggiore) Self-researched

Cesare Nebbia Fresco 1587–1589

The Sistine Chapel of Santa Maria Maggiore (not to be confused with the Vatican Sistine Chapel) was commissioned by Pope Sixtus V and designed by Domenico Fontana. The interior frescoes were directed by Cesare Nebbia and Giovanni Guerra, depicting scenes from the life of the Virgin and Christ's childhood. The dome fresco shows the Assumption of the Virgin. The chapel also houses the tomb of Pope Sixtus V and an ancient relic believed to be pieces of the crib of the Nativity.
