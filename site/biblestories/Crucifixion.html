Crucifixion

Wikipedia

Summary

The Crucifixion depicts the death of Jesus Christ on the cross at Golgotha (also called Calvary). After being condemned by Pontius Pilate, Jesus was scourged, crowned with thorns, and forced to carry his cross to the place of execution. He was crucified between two criminals. During the crucifixion, darkness covered the land for three hours, and at the moment of his death, Jesus cried out "It is finished." The temple veil was torn in two, and an earthquake occurred. A Roman centurion declared "Truly this was the Son of God." This central event in Christian theology represents Christ's sacrifice for the redemption of humanity's sins.

Crucifix

Giotto di Bondone Tempera on panel c. 1290–1300

The earliest known artwork by Giotto. Revolutionary in its naturalistic depiction of Christ's suffering body, departing from the rigid Byzantine tradition.

Brunelleschi Crucifix Self-researched

Filippo Brunelleschi Polychrome painted pearwood c. 1410-1415

A polychrome painted wooden sculpture located in the Gondi Chapel at the church of Santa Maria Novella. This idealized depiction of the crucifixion of Jesus measures around 170 cm × 170 cm. This is the only surviving wooden sculpture by Brunelleschi; the only other known example, a wooden sculpture of Mary Magdalene at the church of Santo Spirito, was destroyed in a fire in 1471. Art historian Luciano Bellosi described Brunelleschi's crucifix as "probably the first Renaissance work in the history of art," representing a definitive turn away from the stylised postures of Gothic sculpture and a return to the naturalism of classical sculpture. According to Vasari, Brunelleschi carved this crucifix in response to Donatello's crucifix at Santa Croce, which Brunelleschi criticized as depicting "a peasant on the Cross."

Bronze Pulpits Self-researched

Donatello Bronze c. 1460-1466

Donatello's final works, these two bronze pulpits in the nave of San Lorenzo depict the Passion and Resurrection of Christ with remarkable emotional intensity. The reliefs show scenes including the Crucifixion, Lamentation, Entombment, and Resurrection. The pulpits were completed by Donatello's workshop after his death in 1466, and some scholars believe the finish shows hands other than the master's. The expressive, almost tortured style of these late works differs markedly from Donatello's earlier, more classical manner. Donatello is buried in San Lorenzo, near Cosimo de' Medici, his longtime patron.

Crucifix

Michelangelo Polychrome wood c. 1492

The only wood work we know for Michelangelo. Created for the church of Santo Spirito in Florence. The slender, delicate figure of Christ differs markedly from Michelangelo's later muscular representations.
