Madonna and Child

Wikipedia

Summary

The Madonna and Child depicts the Virgin Mary with the infant Jesus. This iconic image represents the Incarnation—God becoming human through Christ's birth to Mary. While not depicting a specific biblical narrative, this devotional image draws from the Gospel accounts of Christ's birth and infancy. The scene emphasizes Mary's role as Theotokos (Mother of God) and the tender relationship between mother and divine child. It became one of the most prevalent subjects in Christian art, particularly in Italian Renaissance painting.

Biblical Reference

Book(s): Gospel of Luke, Gospel of Matthew

Chapters/Verses: Luke 2:1-20, Matthew 1:18-25

Madonna with Glass Eyes (Madonna degli Occhi di Vetro) Self-researched

Arnolfo di Cambio Marble with glass inlay c. 1300-1310

An original sculpture from the main portal of Florence Cathedral, depicting the Virgin Mary with Saints Reparata and Zanobi. Notable for the glass eyes that give the figure an unusually lifelike appearance. The use of glass inlay for the eyes was an innovative technique that enhanced the sculpture's expressiveness.

Madonna of the Magnificat

Sandro Botticelli Tempera on panel c. 1481

Probably a wedding present or a gift for a birth of a child. The circular format (tondo) was popular for domestic devotional images. Changing size from 33 to 46 inches was typical at the time.

Madonna of the Stairs

Michelangelo Marble relief c. 1491

An early relief sculpture by Michelangelo, created when he was about 16 years old. Notable for the extraordinary muscular back and right arm of the Christ Child, foreshadowing Michelangelo's later mastery of the human form. The technique shows the influence of Donatello's schiacciato (very shallow relief), which Michelangelo studied during his time in the Medici garden. Despite its small size (55.5 x 40 cm), the composition feels monumental, with the Virgin's powerful, muscular arms and contemplative pose anticipating Michelangelo's mature style. Children play on stairs in the background—hence the name.

The Doni Tondo

Michelangelo Tempera on panel c. 1506

The only preserved panel painting of Michelangelo. Also known as "The Dono Madonna" or "Holy Family with St. John the Baptist," this tondo (circular painting) was commissioned by the wealthy Florentine merchant Agnolo Doni. The unusual poses and vibrant colors influenced later Mannerist painters.

Pesaro Madonna Self-researched

Titian Oil on canvas 1519-1526

Located in the left nave, this revolutionary painting places the Madonna and Child dramatically off-center to the upper right rather than in the physical center. Commissioned by Bishop Jacopo Pesaro, this asymmetrical arrangement was highly innovative and influenced religious painting for generations.

Madonna and Child with Angels Self-researched

Lorenzo Lotto Oil on panel c. 1525

Spiritual, meditative figures set in a landscape. Lotto's distinctive psychological approach to religious subjects is evident in this work.

Madonna and Child with Saint Anne (Dei Palafrenieri) Self-researched

Caravaggio Oil on canvas 1605–1606

Painted for the altar of the Papal Grooms in St. Peter's Basilica but quickly removed due to its unorthodox portrayal, including the nudity of the Christ child. Purchased by Cardinal Scipione Borghese after its rejection.

Madonna and Child with St. Nicholas and St. Lawrence Self-researched
